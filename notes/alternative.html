Carrying Cost = Foward Price - Spot Commodity Price

the cost incurred as a result of an investment position

In the commodities markets, the cost of carry includes the cost of necessary insurances and the expense of storing the physical commodity over a period of time
In capital markets, the cost of carry is the difference between the yield generated from the security and the cost of entering and maintaining the position.

calendar spreads summary

Calendar spreads that contain long and short positions of equal notional value are hedged against changes in the spot price.
Changes in the spot price (everything else being equal) may be viewed as causing a parallel or additive shift in the entire term structure of forward prices.
Changes in the costs of carry cause a slope change in the term structure of forward prices.
Returns on calendar spreads are primarily driven by two equivalent concepts:
changes in the slope of the term structure of forward prices
changes in carrying costs.

Relative value fund managers take long and short positions that are relatively equal in size, volatility, and other risk exposures

The best known was LTCM

Four main types includes:

  1. convertible bond arbitrage
  2. volatility arbitrage
  3. fixed-income arbitrage
  4. relative value multi-strategy funds

Notes:

  • The classic convertible bond arbitrage trade is to purchase a convertible bond that is believed to be undervalued and to hedge its risk using a short position in the underlying equity.
$$\text{Convertible Bond Price} = \text{Value of Straight Corporate Debt} + \text{Value of the Implicit Equity Call Option}$$ $$\text{Conversion Ratio} = \text{Number of Shares per Convertible Bond}$$ $$\text{Option Strike Price} = \frac{\text{Convertible Bond Face Value}}{\text{Conversion Ratio}}$$ $$\text{Conversion Value} = \text{Current Stock Price} \times \text{Conversion Ratio}$$ $$\text{Conversion Premium} = \frac{(\text{Convertible Bond Price} - \text{Conversion Value})}{\text{Conversion Value}}$$

Example:
Consider a firm with a borrowing cost of 8% on unsecured, subordinated straight debt and a current stock price of $40. The firm may be able to issue three-year convertible bonds at an annual coupon rate of 4% by offering a conversion ratio such as 20.
Given that a strike price of $50, and other parameters, such as volatility and dividends—is valued at $5.14 per share according to the Black-Scholes option pricing model.
what are the value of the convertible bond, the conversion value, and the conversion premium?

Answer:
Value of straight debt:40/1.08+...+1040/1.08^3=896.92
Value of the implicit equity call option=5.14*20=102.8
Convertible bond price=896.92+102.8=999.72

  • Bonds with very high conversion premiums are often called busted convertibles, as the embedded stock options are far out-of-the-money. (bond-like convertible)
  • Bonds with very low conversion premiums have stock options that are deep in- the-money, where the convertible bond price and the conversion value are very close. (equity-like convertible)
  • Convertible bonds with moderately sized conversion ratios have stock options closer to being at-the- money and are called hybrid convertibles.

Equity hedge funds follow the most popular hedge fund strategy, whether measured in terms of assets under management (AUM) or in terms of the number of funds.

    tend to have net positive systematic risk exposure from taking a net long position

  • Equity market-neutral funds
    attempt to balance short and long positions, ideally matching the beta exposure
  • short-bias funds
    have larger short positions than long positions

Source of return (Equity Hedge Fund)

  • Providing liquidity
  • providing information efficiency
  • identifying company profit opportunities

Providing liquidity

Liquidity in this context is the extent to which transactions can be executed with minimal disruption to prices.
Many hedge fund managers provide liquidity by searching markets to detect price movements that appear to be driven by orders that are large relative to existing liquidity
Providing liquidity refers to the placement of limit orders or other actions that increase the number of shares available to be bought or sold near the current best bid and offer prices

Providing information efficiency

Abnormal profit opportunities tend to come from market inefficiencies, and market inefficiencies tend to come from reduced competition.
Another potential source of abnormal profits for hedge funds is overreacting/ underreacting

Identifying the factor that could create profit opportunities

Some equity hedge fund managers analyze the factors that drive the equity returns of each company in search of those that offer the ability to predict the equities that offer ex ante alpha.
Single factor model: CAPM(注：CAPM不是基于因子模型。。。)
Fama and French (1993): three-factor model

Market Anomalies

Investment strategies that can be identified based on available information and that offer higher expected returns after adjustment for risk are known as market anomalies, and they are violations of informational market efficiency.

  • Some observed anomalies in market efficiency
    1. Accounting accruals(is the recognition of a value based on anticipation of a transaction)
    2. Price momentum
    3. Earning momentum
    4. Net stock issuance(Prior studies show that firms with positive net stock issuance (i.e. stock issues) have lower future stock returns)
    5. Insider trading(Insider trading can be illegal or legal depending on when the insider makes the trade, Legal insider trading happens when directors of the company purchase or sell shares, but they disclose their transactions legally.)
    $$SUE=\frac{EPS-\text{Analyst Consensus EPS Estimate}}{\text{Standard Deviation of Earnings Surprises}}$$

    Law of active management

    The breadth of a strategy is the number of independent active bets placed into an active portfolio.

    • A bet is simply a position in a security that is established because the security is perceived to be mispriced.
    • Independent refers to the statistical lack of correlation between the bets.
    • Active refers to the relatively short-term nature of the perceived return advantage.

    Calculate MOIC

    1. purchase price of target firm
    2. debt and equity funding amount in the purchase price
    3. levered Free cash Flow
    4. ending purchase price (exit equity value) and MOIC
    $$\begin{aligned} &\text{EBITDA}\\ -&\text{D \& A}\\\hline =&\text{EBIT}\\ -&\text{Interest}\\\hline =&\text{EBT}\\ -&\text{Tax}\\\hline =&\text{Net Income}\\ +&\text{D \& A}\\ -&\text{CapEx}\\ -&\text{Change(+) in NWC}\\\hline =&\text{LFCF}\\ \end{aligned}$$
    $$\text{Exit Equity Value}=\text{Enterprise Value}-\text{transaction debt}+\text{LFCF}$$ $$MOIC\text{(Multiple on Invested Capital)}=\frac{\text{realized value}}{\text{total dollar amount invested}}$$
    1. Enterprise value: ending purchase price
    2. transaction debt: beginning debt from bank
    3. LFCF : sum of Free Cash Flow from the first period to the last period.

    You are now a PE Associate and you have to figure out the expected returns on an investment.
    The company has revenue of $100 in Year 0, growing $10 every year. It has an EBITDA of $50 in Year 0, also growing at $10 every year.
    We’re buying it at the end of Year 0 for 10x LTM EBITDA (EBITDA multiple=10x). The debt-to-equity ratio for the LBO acquisition will be 50:50.
    Suppose D&A is expected to equal $10, Capital Expenditures are expected to equal $10 , and operating working capital (NWC) is expected to increase by $5 each year, staying constant through our investment period. Interest rate is 10% and tax rate is 40%.
    We exit the investment in Year 5 at the same EBITDA multiple used as at entry, what’s the exit equity value, MOIC?

    Answer:purchase price:10*50=500, debt=500*50/(50+50)=250, equity=250, EBITDA_5=100,sell price=100*10=1000, LFCF=10+16.5+22+28+34=110, exit quity value(Ending purchase price)=1000-250+110=860
    MOIC=860/250=3.4
