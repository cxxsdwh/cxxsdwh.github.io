费马引理(Fermat's theorem (stationary points))

Let \(f:(a,b)\to R\) be a function and suppose that \(x_0\in (a,b)\) is a point where \(f\) has a local extremum. If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x_0\)

Then \(f'(x_0)=0\)

Proof1: Non-vanishing derivatives implies not extremum

assume without loss of generality that \(K>0\)

$$f'(x_0)=K\text{ means that}$$ $$\lim\limits_{\varepsilon\rarr 0}\frac{f(x_0+\varepsilon)-f(x_0)}{\varepsilon}=K$$

In particular, for sufficiently small \(\varepsilon\) (less than some \(\varepsilon_0\)

$$\frac{f(x_0+\varepsilon)-f(x_0)}{\varepsilon}>K/2$$

If \(\varepsilon>0\), then

$$f(x_0+\varepsilon)>f(x_0)+(K/2)\varepsilon>f(x_0)$$

If \(\varepsilon<0\), then

$$f(x_0+\varepsilon)\lt f(x_0)+(K/2)\varepsilon\lt f(x_0)$$

Thus \(x_{0}\) is not a local or global maximum or minimum of \(f\).
